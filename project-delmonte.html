Overview

The owners of this 1994 River Oaks home loved everything but the kitchen — a dim room of cherry cabinetry and tiled granite that turned its back on the garden and forced the cook into a corner. They cooked daily and entertained often, yet the space worked against both. Over eighteen weeks we took the room to the studs, annexed square footage from an oversized formal dining room, and rebuilt it as one gracious culinary space, with a discreet scullery for the work guests never need to see.

What We Did

The brief was deceptively simple: make the kitchen the heart of the house without enlarging the home's footprint. The existing room was boxed in on three sides, lit by a single window and a fluorescent panel, and separated from both the garden and the family by a load-bearing wall. The clients wanted light, a proper island for two cooks, and somewhere to hide the coffee maker, the small appliances, and the inevitable mess of real entertaining.

The Approach

Our design team reclaimed roughly 140 square feet from a formal dining room the family never used, then removed the wall between the two spaces with a concealed steel beam so the new kitchen could open to the garden through a wider run of glass. The plan placed a single 13-foot island at the center, wrapped the perimeter in rift-sawn white oak, and tucked a full scullery — second sink, dishwasher, and the everyday appliances — behind a cased opening just out of sightline.

Materials were chosen to feel collected rather than decorated: book-matched Calacatta Viola on the island and backsplash, a hand-troweled plaster hood, unlacquered brass that will warm with use, and integrated, fully paneled appliances so the room reads as furniture, not a showroom. Every run was milled in our own shop to the millimeter.

"We finally have a kitchen that works as hard as we do — and somehow still looks calm when forty people are over. Not one surprise in eighteen weeks."

The Result

What was a dark, single-cook galley is now a light-filled room that anchors the main floor. The garden reads as part of the kitchen, two people can cook without colliding, and the scullery absorbs the clutter that used to live on the counters. It is, by the owners' account, the first room guests gravitate to and the last they leave.

Scope of Work

  • Full demolition to the studs and annexation of an adjacent formal dining room
  • Concealed structural steel to remove a load-bearing wall
  • Widened rear glazing to open the kitchen to the garden
  • Custom rift-sawn white oak cabinetry milled in our shop
  • Book-matched Calacatta Viola island, counters, and full-height backsplash
  • Hand-troweled plaster range hood and unlacquered brass hardware
  • Concealed scullery with second sink, dishwasher, and appliance garage
  • New electrical, plumbing, and layered architectural lighting
